Kansas City, Missouri — 1940s to 1970s — The Slow Undoing of the Pre-War City

The Postwar Pause

The 1940s arrive in Kansas City with the civic infrastructure of the Boom Era still intact and largely new. City Hall is not yet ten years old. The Municipal Auditorium is five. The Power & Light Building, Fidelity Bank, the Jackson County Courthouse — all these buildings are visibly current architecture in 1945, the way the Kauffman Center feels current in 2026. The skyline is complete. The boulevards work. The streetcar runs. The civic institutions are young and confident. By 1970, very little of that will still be true in the same way.

Wartime Kansas City was a busy place. The defense industries expanded; downtown was vital as an office and retail center; Truman was a local boy who had made good in the Senate and was about to become President. The end of the war brought a pause in major new construction — almost nothing substantial was built between 1940 and the mid-1950s — but the city that existed continued to function as a city. The slow undoing came later.

◆

The 1950s as Transition

Three events in a single decade set the trajectory for everything that followed. In July 1951, the Great Flood filled the West Bottoms with thirty-five feet of water and ended the district's commercial viability as an industrial center. The stockyards were already in decline; the flood accelerated the decline by ending the trust that the flood-prone Bottoms could be a reliable location for major businesses. The West Bottoms would spend the next forty years as a mostly-abandoned industrial landscape before the 21st century arts and antiques revival began.  Read about the Great Flood →

In June 1957, the last Kansas City streetcar made its final run. The system that had shaped the metropolitan geography for seventy years was dismantled over the next few seasons. The bus-and-car city that replaced it was more flexible in some ways and catastrophically different in others — the density that the streetcar corridors had supported would not be sustained by automobile-dependent infrastructure.  Read about the streetcar →

And through the 1950s, the first federal highway projects began cutting through the city. The Interstate Highway Act of 1956 authorized the freeway system that would, in Kansas City as in every other American city, divide neighborhoods, sever commercial streets from their foot traffic, and make automobile commuting from suburbs practical at a scale not previously possible. Prairie Village, Raytown, Fairway, Overland Park — the southern and western suburbs began their growth spurts in this period, drawing families and businesses out of the central city.

◆

The 1960s: Urban Renewal Accelerates

The federal Housing Acts of 1949 and 1954 funded what the country called “urban renewal” — the large-scale demolition of areas designated as blighted, usually to make way for public projects or modernist redevelopment. In Kansas City, the demolitions fell predominantly on Black neighborhoods east of Troost Avenue. Interstate 70 was routed through the East Side on paths chosen over less destructive alternatives. Entire blocks of 18th and Vine were cleared. The Black business district that had anchored Kansas City's jazz era was substantially gone by the end of the decade.  Read about Urban Renewal →

The 1968 riots — the four-day unrest after Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination — concentrated in the neighborhoods east of Troost and accelerated the physical damage that the demolitions had already started. What had been a dense, economically viable Black commercial district in 1950 was, by 1970, a landscape of cleared lots, partial demolitions, and scattered surviving buildings.  Read about the 1968 Riots →

Downtown was hollowing out from the other side. The department stores and specialty retailers that had occupied the 1920s and 30s commercial blocks began closing or moving to the Plaza and the suburban shopping centers. Office workers still filled downtown during business hours, but the after-hours city was quieter every year. By 1970 you could walk through downtown at six in the evening and have the streets largely to yourself.

◆

The 1970s: The Hollowed-Out Decade

By the early 1970s the pattern was clear. The central business district had lost roughly half its population and a larger share of its commercial vitality. Union Station stopped serving passenger trains in 1970, shuttered most of its interior spaces by 1985, and spent the next three decades as a mostly-empty monument to an earlier civic ambition. The great Art Deco office buildings were still mostly occupied, but at lower rents and by less prestigious tenants than in their first decades. Quality Hill had lost most of its wealthy residents and was reaching toward the condition from which it would later have to be rescued by preservation.

What the decade did build was suburban and automotive. The Truman Sports Complex opened in 1972 and 1973: Arrowhead Stadium for the Chiefs, Kauffman Stadium for the Royals (then the Kansas City Royals), both on a single site at the I-70/I-435 intersection far east of downtown. The stadium complex was a genuinely new piece of civic architecture — two major-league stadiums on a shared parking-lot campus, designed for automobile access. It was also a statement that the civic energy of the 1970s would not be concentrated downtown.

The Plaza continued to thrive. Crown Center, developed by Hallmark around its headquarters in the late 1960s and through the 1970s, became an alternative downtown with its own hotel, shopping, and residential components. These were the commercial centers that survived the decade with their vitality intact. The historic downtown did not.

◆

The First Glimmers of Preservation

By the late 1970s, the same forces that had hollowed out downtown had created the conditions for its eventual recovery. Real estate was cheap. The surviving Art Deco buildings were preserved by neglect — no one had torn them down because no one was building anything new. The Landmarks Commission, established in 1970, began designating historic districts. The first restoration projects in the Crossroads took shape. The National Register of Historic Places added Kansas City buildings by the dozen through the late 1970s.

The 1980s would bring the first new skyscrapers since 1937 — Town Pavilion in 1986, One Kansas City Place in 1988. The recovery would be long, uneven, and in many ways incomplete. But the mid-century story — from 1945 to 1980, roughly — is the story of how a city that had built extraordinary things in the 1920s and 30s spent the next four decades watching much of what it had built be diminished, and then beginning, tentatively, to imagine how some of what was lost might be restored.
