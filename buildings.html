A City That Built to Last

Kansas City's architectural story unfolds in three distinct chapters: a downtown skyline of extraordinary coherence born from a single decade of civic ambition, a modern era of second-wave boldness, and a century of sports venues that mark the city's evolving civic identity. Each era has a character of its own — and all of it is still standing.

1900s – 1940s

Boom Era

The Ten-Year Plan, the Pendergast machine, and a decade of national prosperity combined to build a downtown skyline of limestone towers that still defines Kansas City.

Explore Boom Era ◆
1970s – Present

Modern Era

After decades of suburban flight, Kansas City resumed building at civic scale — a Moshe Safdie concert hall, a convention center that spans an interstate, and an arena built on faith alone.

Explore Modern Era ◆
1972 – 2024

Stadiums

From the world's loudest outdoor stadium to the first venue purpose-built for a women's professional sports team — Kansas City builds sports architecture with the same intention as its civic buildings.

Three Buildings to Know

If you only have time for three, make it these.

KC Power & Light Building

1931 • Art Deco • 476 ft • Boom Era

Thirty-six floors of limestone and bronze, its crown lit gold every night — the defining beacon of the Kansas City skyline for nearly a century.

Read More ◆

Kauffman Center

2011 • Moshe Safdie • Modern Era

A sweeping stainless-steel shell housing two world-class halls — the most architecturally ambitious building in Kansas City since City Hall went up in 1937.

Read More ◆

CPKC Stadium

2024 • Generator Studio • Stadiums

The first stadium in the world purpose-built for a women's professional sports team — privately financed, on the Missouri River, a statement about what a city believes.
