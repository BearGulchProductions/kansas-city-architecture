Sources &
Scholarship

The books, archives, and research that made this site possible.

Every essay on this site draws on a tradition of scholarship, local knowledge, and primary-source research. This page documents the sources that made Look Up, Kansas City possible — books, archives, tours, and conversations. For a reader-friendly annotated bibliography, see Further Reading.

◆

Books

The books below are the backbone of the site. The fuller annotated bibliography lives on the Reading page.

  • William S. Worley, J.C. Nichols and the Shaping of Kansas City (University of Missouri Press, 1990). The primary source for Nichols Legacy, the Country Club District coverage, and the restrictive-covenant analysis.
  • Sherry Lamb Schirmer, A City Divided: The Racial Landscape of Kansas City, 1900–1960 (University of Missouri Press, 2002). The essential account for Race & the City, the Troost line, and the 18th & Vine coverage.
  • Lawrence H. Larsen & Nancy J. Hulston, Pendergast! (University of Missouri Press, 1997). The standard Pendergast biography; source for the Pendergast page and the Boom Era coverage.
  • Frank W. Driggs & Chuck Haddix, Kansas City Jazz: From Ragtime to Bebop (Oxford University Press, 2005). The primary source for Jazz & Architecture and Buck O'Neil.
  • Ehrlich & Garney, Kansas City, Missouri: An Architectural History (University of Missouri Press, 1992). Foundational for the buildings coverage and architectural style pages.
  • Monroe Dodd, A Splendid Ride: The Streetcars of Kansas City, 1870–1957 (Kansas City Star Books, 2002). The source for the historic streetcar system.
  • John Mack Faragher et al., Out of Many: A History of the American People (various editions). General American historical context.
  • Jason Roe & Christopher Wolff, Kansas City: Then & Now (Arcadia, 2017). Useful for Lost Kansas City comparisons.
  • Rick Montgomery & Shirl Kasper, Kansas City: An American Story (Kansas City Star Books, 1999). Comprehensive general history, used across eras pages.

Archives

  • Missouri Valley Special Collections, Kansas City Public Library (Central Library, 14 W. 10th St.). The single most important archive for Kansas City history; holds the largest collection of historical KC photography, city records, and local newspapers. Research access is free and the staff is extraordinary.
  • The Kansas City Museum (3218 Gladstone Blvd.). Long-form curatorial collections on civic history, Pendergast-era materials, stockyards records, neighborhood ephemera.
  • Linda Hall Library (5109 Cherry St.). Science and engineering archive with significant holdings on the railroad era, bridge engineering, and industrial history relevant to the West Bottoms coverage.
  • UMKC Miller Nichols Library, Special Collections. LaBudde Special Collections holds the papers of J.C. Nichols and other developers; essential source material for the Nichols Legacy page.
  • Black Archives of Mid-America (1722 E. 17th Terrace). Primary-source archive on the Black history of Kansas City, including Negro Leagues baseball materials and 18th & Vine district records.

Walking Tours & Guides

  • Kansas City Architectural Foundation (kcarchfoundation.org). Annual Open House Kansas City (usually October) and occasional guided tours of individual buildings.
  • Historic Kansas City Foundation (historickansascity.org). Advocacy, preservation, and occasional programming. Useful resource for understanding the preservation movement's history in KC.
  • KC Parks self-guided boulevard and fountain tours. Free downloadable PDFs on kcparks.org.
  • Monroe Dodd, A Shifting Skyline: Kansas City's Architectural Heritage (Kansas City Star Books, various). Self-guided tour format informed multiple walking tour pages on this site.

Primary Source Photography

  • Missouri Valley Special Collections (KCPL). Historical photographs of KC from the 1870s forward — the richest single source of period imagery.
  • Wikimedia Commons. The current-era photography used on this site comes primarily from Wikimedia contributors. Image credits live at credits.html.
  • Original photography is in progress; as it is added to the site, the affected pages will have the .image-detail component and photographer credit.

A Note on Methodology

This site attempts to be accurate. Where sources conflict, we try to note it. Where we've made editorial judgments, we try to say so. Errors are inevitable in a project this size — if you find one, please be in touch.

The standard editorial approach is to hold the civic history and the architectural history in one view. That means we don't separate the beauty of the Country Club Plaza from the racial covenants J.C. Nichols wrote into its deeds; we don't separate the Pendergast era's Art Deco achievements from its corruption; we don't separate the 18th & Vine cultural heritage from the urban renewal demolitions that diminished it. The blog post on honest history is the longest statement of this approach.
