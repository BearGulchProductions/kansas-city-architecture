A Walk Through the Crossroads at Midnight

After the First Friday crowds leave, the warehouses fall back into their older quiet. A walk from 20th and Baltimore up to the edge of downtown.

First Friday ends sometime around eleven. The last gallery has turned its lights down, the food trucks have packed up, the line outside the bar at 19th and Wyandotte has thinned to a few regulars. By midnight the Crossroads empties fast. You can stand on a corner that had two thousand people on it three hours earlier and hear nothing but the HVAC on the roof of a warehouse across the street. It's one of the distinctive qualities of the district — how quickly it goes from festival to near-silence, and how the architecture looks different in each version.

I walk north from 20th and Baltimore. The buildings on these blocks are almost all late nineteenth-century and early twentieth-century industrial — brick piers, big arched loading-dock openings, freight elevators visible behind glass. Most of them were warehouses. Some of them were small manufacturing. A few were commercial with warehouse uses on the upper floors. The Crossroads was the wholesaling district for a city whose economy ran on moving things between rail and truck, and the buildings were built for that. They have steel frames inside the brick skin. They have twelve-foot ceilings and wood floors that were rated for machine loads. They were overbuilt for what they needed to do, because in 1905 nobody expected to tear them down.

At night they do a thing that the district's daytime version doesn't show. The brick absorbs the streetlight differently than limestone does. Limestone picks up the light evenly across its surface and goes pale. Brick catches the light in the mortar joints first, then in the fired faces of each individual unit, and ends up reading as a textured field of small warm highlights — you can see the hand of the bricklayer from a hundred years ago, the slight irregularities in the coursing, the places where the wall has been patched. Downtown's towers go monumental at night because their stone is continuous. The Crossroads stays granular because the brick never stops being brick.

Turn east on 18th and you walk into a pool of quieter blocks. The big galleries are further west; the renovated loft buildings are further west; the coffee shops with their windows full of houseplants are further west. Here the streetscape is warehouses that still look like warehouses, a couple of print shops, a tile distributor. At night they look like they did in 1935. The only clue that it is not 1935 is the streetlight, which is LED now, and the parked cars, which have gotten rounder.

Then I cross 17th and the view opens up to the north. And there it is: the illuminated crown of the Power & Light Building, eight blocks away at 14th and Baltimore, floating above the nearer roofs. The crown has been lit in different colors over the years — blue, red, white, gold — but the geometry of the light is the same. Six stepped setbacks, each thinner than the one below, terminating in the open observation deck that used to be public. From the Crossroads at midnight, the crown reads as a small city of its own, suspended a few hundred feet up, hovering over the darker bulk of the downtown towers. Every time I see it from this angle I understand why the building was the anchor of the 1931 skyline and why it still is. There is no other building in Kansas City that produces this effect.

Past the P&L, you can see the top edge of 909 Walnut (also called Fidelity Bank), which has its own Art Deco crown, less dramatic than the P&L's but cut from the same moment. You can see the square top of One Kansas City Place catching some ambient light off the clouds. You can just about see the pyramid on City Hall if you know where to look. The whole deco skyline, visible from a warehouse district built a generation earlier by the same civic class that would later build the skyline you are watching. You are looking at two chapters of Kansas City architecture from the vantage of a third.

Jane Jacobs called city life "the ballet of the sidewalk," and the phrase usually gets applied to daytime streets — the woman walking her dog, the kids coming home from school, the shop owner sweeping in front of his door. It is a real thing. It is the version of the city that gets written about most often because it is the version that looks most alive. But there is another version, the one that happens between midnight and four in the morning, when the ballet has gone home and the architecture is on its own. The buildings look more like themselves in this version. They are not performing for anyone. The Crossroads brick is brick; the P&L crown is lit because the timers say so, not because anyone is watching. If you walk through the district at that hour what you are seeing is the city without the people — the set without the performance. It is a useful way to see a place you think you know, because it refuses to sell you anything. It just sits there, stone and brick and light, exactly what it is.

The walk ends for me at the corner of 16th and Baltimore, at the foot of the slope that becomes downtown. Above me the P&L crown is still there. It will stay lit until dawn. I turn around and walk back south into the quiet. Somewhere a garbage truck has started working one block over. First Friday is a week away again.
