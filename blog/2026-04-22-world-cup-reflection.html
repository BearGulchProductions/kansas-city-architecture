What the World Cup Tells Us About Kansas City

The tournament is the current instance of a recurring civic impulse. What matters is what the city does with the attention after it leaves.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup arrives in Kansas City in about seven weeks. Six matches at Arrowhead Stadium, including a quarter-final. Estimates of visitor numbers keep revising upward: 650,000 is the current working figure, across the tournament window, and nobody really knows. Hotels are booked. Airbnbs are booked. The Streetcar extension to the Berkley Riverfront is expected to open in time. The Plaza has been getting quiet surface-level improvements for two years. The Crossroads is bracing for a month of foot traffic it has never experienced before. If you live here, the tournament has already changed the city in ways too small to tally. It hasn't yet happened and it has already happened.

I want to make an argument about what the World Cup is, underneath the obvious bracket of "a soccer tournament happening here." It is the current instance of a pattern the site has a name for: civic imagination — Kansas City's recurring impulse to imagine itself larger than it is, and to build something permanent during the reach. The 1900 Convention Hall was built in ninety days to host a national political convention. The 1928 Ten-Year Plan rebuilt the civic infrastructure during a period of booster confidence that felt less like planning than like a dare to the future. The 1999 Union Station reopening was a bi-state sales tax passed because the city had decided it wasn't going to be the kind of city that lost its Union Station. The World Cup, seen against that lineage, is the current episode of the same impulse. The tournament is the pretext. The pretext matters less than what gets built while the city is paying attention.

And this is where the question gets harder. What has gotten built for the World Cup? The Streetcar north extension, which would probably have happened anyway but happened faster. The Plaza resurfacing. Airport improvements that were scheduled regardless. A new hotel or two downtown. CPKC Stadium, which technically predates the World Cup announcement but exists in the same moment of riverfront investment. The inventory is respectable but not transformative. Compare it to the 1928 list and the asymmetry is stark: City Hall, the Jackson County Courthouse, the Municipal Auditorium, expanded Liberty Memorial grounds, a reshaped downtown street grid. The Ten-Year Plan built a city. The World Cup is polishing one.

That's not a complaint. The Ten-Year Plan happened in a city that could still think in decades, and was funded by a political machine that doesn't exist any more, and built some of what it built using Black labor displaced by the same plan. The absence of a 2020s equivalent isn't a failure of ambition; it's a different set of constraints. But it does mean the honest version of the World Cup story isn't "look at what we built for the world." It's closer to: we happened to have a ready-made stadium far from downtown, built for a different sport in a different era, and we are hosting the world in it while the neighborhoods between the stadium and downtown continue to do the work of recovering from a century of different decisions.

The narrative risk is what I'm actually worried about. The World Cup will produce a set of stories about Kansas City that outlast the tournament by decades. Sports writers will parachute in, eat at a list of pre-vetted restaurants, write a barbecue paragraph and a jazz paragraph and a Plaza paragraph, and leave. The version of Kansas City that becomes the national shorthand will be built from those paragraphs. It will center the experience of a visiting fan in an entertainment district. It will not mention Troost. It will not mention the 1968 riots or the neighborhoods east of Prospect that are still visibly carrying the weight of the urban renewal decades. It will not mention that the Kauffman Center sits two miles from census tracts where median household income is one-third the city average. It doesn't have to mention any of this for the tournament to go well. But the shorthand gets written while everyone is watching, and then the shorthand is what gets repeated.

My specific wish for the post-World Cup city: that the infrastructure built for visitors becomes infrastructure used by residents, and that the parts of the city that hosted the tournament quietly — the neighborhoods the shuttles drove past, the blocks that rented their spare rooms — see some of the attention turn into sustained investment. My specific concern: that the post-tournament period looks a lot like the post-1928 period — a city that reached, built something real, and then spent the next four decades slowly pulling back from the edges of what it had built. That's happened here before. The difference this time would have to be deliberate. I don't think it's automatic. I don't think the tournament alone will make it happen.

Still — and this is the part the site is committed to holding alongside the critique — something is about to happen here that very few American cities ever get to have happen. People who have never thought about Kansas City are about to think about it for a month. The city is going to put on a version of itself that has been rehearsed for three years. Some of that version is real. The Art Deco skyline is real. The fountains are real. The jazz history is real, even where the blocks that produced it have been scraped down to parking lots. The Kauffman Center is real. The barbecue is real. If the city tells the whole truth — the built beauty alongside the buried cost — the tournament will matter long after the last match. If it only tells half the truth, the other half will still be here in August 2026, and the decade after that, waiting.
