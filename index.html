A City Built in Gold

Kansas City rose to prominence during the Art Deco era of the 1920s and 1930s, leaving behind a skyline rich with geometric ornament, gleaming terra cotta, and monumental ambition. From the soaring spires of its downtown towers to the grand civic halls that line its boulevards, KC is one of America's great repositories of Deco design.

This site is a tribute to those buildings — the architects who dreamed them, the craftsmen who built them, and the city that preserved them.

Notable Buildings

Kansas City Power & Light Building

1931

A thirty-six-story tower of limestone and bronze, its crown illuminated nightly — a beacon of Deco verticality on the KC skyline.

Municipal Auditorium

1935

One of the finest civic Art Deco buildings in the nation, featuring sweeping geometric reliefs and a grand arena that still hosts events today.

Fidelity Bank Building

1931

Distinguished by its ornate terracotta facade and dramatically setback upper floors — a textbook example of Deco massing and decoration.

Union Station

1914 / Restored 1999

A Beaux-Arts giant that anchors the southern approach to downtown, its grand hall restored to glory and now a cultural destination.

City Hall

1937

Thirty floors of Indiana limestone rise in a bold stepped silhouette — the seat of city government and one of KC's most photographed Deco landmarks.

Midland Theatre

1927

An opulent atmospheric theater whose gilded interior transports audiences to a vanished golden age of entertainment and civic pride.

City of Fountains

Kansas City is home to more than 200 fountains — more per capita than any city in the world except Rome. From grand civic monuments to intimate neighborhood sculptures, water has long shaped the character of Kansas City's public spaces.

J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain

Installed 1960 • Mill Creek Park

Four larger-than-life bronze horsemen — representing the world's great rivers — preside over KC's most beloved fountain. The sculpture was cast in 1910 by French artist Henri-Léon Gréber and installed at the east edge of the Country Club Plaza.

Meyer Circle Sea Horse Fountain

Early 1920s • Ward Parkway

A 17th-century Venetian bronze acquired by developer J.C. Nichols in the 1920s, this seahorse sculpture anchors the graceful Ward Parkway traffic circle at Meyer Boulevard — a jewel of KC's renowned boulevard system.

Firefighters Fountain

1991 • Penn Valley Park

Dedicated to the six Kansas City firefighters killed in the catastrophic Institutional Distribution warehouse fire of November 1988, this solemn monument in Penn Valley Park combines cascading water with bronze relief work to honor their sacrifice.

William Volker Memorial Fountain

1958 • Volker Boulevard

Honoring Kansas City's great philanthropist — known as "Mr. Kansas City" — this cascading waterfall fountain anchors the Volker Boulevard median, one of the city's grandest tree-lined parkways near Brush Creek.

What Is Art Deco?

Art Deco emerged in the 1920s as a bold reaction to the organic curves of Art Nouveau. It embraced symmetry, geometry, and the materials of the Machine Age — polished metals, glass, and richly colored stone. Buildings took on a vertical drama, with setbacks that tapered toward the sky and facades encrusted with stylized sunbursts, chevrons, and bas-relief figures.

◆ ▲ ◆ ▲ ◆ ▲ ◆

In Kansas City, Deco arrived at a moment of explosive civic ambition. The city's Ten-Year Plan of 1925 funded a wave of public construction — auditoriums, courthouses, and boulevards — that gave local architects a canvas to express the era's optimism in stone and steel.
