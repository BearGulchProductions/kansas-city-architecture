Ongoing Events & Organizations

What's
Happening

Kansas City has an active architectural culture outside this site. Here's where to find it.

These are the organizations, events, and resources worth knowing about if you want to engage with Kansas City architecture beyond reading. Most are free or modestly priced. Almost all welcome newcomers. This page will be updated as new events and groups become active.

◆

Annual Events

  • Open House Kansas City — usually the second weekend of October. The Kansas City Architectural Foundation's annual public-access day, opening dozens of significant buildings (office towers, private mansions, restoration projects) to the public for free tours.
  • First Fridays in the Crossroads — the first Friday evening of every month. Dozens of galleries open their doors for openings, receptions, and foot traffic. The most reliable way to experience the Crossroads as an arts district rather than just a neighborhood.
  • Fountain Day — opening day of Kansas City's fountain season, usually the second Tuesday in April. Ceremonies at the J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain and Meyer Circle Sea Horse, with the city's 200+ public fountains turning on together for the first time after the winter.
  • Plaza Lighting Ceremony — the evening of Thanksgiving Day. The annual switch-on of the Plaza's holiday lights (roughly 80 miles of bulbs outlining the Spanish Colonial buildings), drawing tens of thousands to the Plaza streets. A signature KC civic ritual since 1930.
  • American Royal BBQ Contest — September, at Kansas Speedway. Not strictly an architecture event, but a peak KC civic occasion worth knowing about.

Ongoing Organizations

  • Kansas City Architectural Foundation (kcarchfoundation.org) — runs Open House KC; advocates for design education; hosts lectures and occasional tours year-round.
  • Historic Kansas City Foundation (historickansascity.org) — preservation advocacy and activism. Maintains the most-endangered historic buildings list for the city. Active in restoration financing and landmarks designations.
  • AIA Kansas City — the local chapter of the American Institute of Architects. Hosts design awards, continuing-education programs, and occasional public-facing events.
  • Kansas City Landmarks Commission — the municipal body that designates local historic landmarks and reviews proposed changes. Meetings are open to the public.
  • The Rehabber Club — the informal community of West Bottoms warehouse restorers and adaptive-reuse developers. Less of an organization than a network; a good way in is the First Weekends events the West Bottoms businesses jointly program.
  • American Jazz Museum and Negro Leagues Baseball Museum (18th & Vine) — the two permanent institutions anchoring the 18th & Vine cultural district. Regular programming around jazz history and civil rights education.
  • National WWI Museum and Memorial (at Liberty Memorial) — the only federally designated National WWI Museum in the United States. Lectures, rotating exhibitions, and the observation deck itself are worth the visit.

Regular Walking Tours

  • Kansas City Architectural Foundation occasional guided tours, usually during shoulder seasons or as part of Open House KC.
  • Historic Kansas City Foundation seasonal guided tours of specific districts — Quality Hill, the Crossroads, Hyde Park. Check the calendar.
  • Self-guided walking tours — this site has four (the Art Deco Mile, the Plaza & Fountains, the West Bottoms, and Union Station to Liberty Memorial).

News Sources Worth Following

  • The Kansas City Star — the paper of record for KC architecture and urbanism coverage. Look for bylines covering city planning, major development projects, and historic preservation.
  • Startland News (startlandnews.com) — covers KC's startup, development, and civic innovation scene.
  • Kansas City Business Journal — deep coverage of commercial real estate and development deals. Good for tracking which buildings are in play.
  • Block Club KC / Startland / local substacks — the independent reporting scene covering neighborhood-level civic issues, preservation fights, and the slow grain of city life.
